# Clinical Guidelines: Hypertension Management

**Source:** 2017 ACC/AHA/AAPA/ABC/ACPM/AGS/APhA/ASH/ASPC/NMA/PCNA Hypertension Guideline  
**Updated by:** AHA/ACC 2023 Resistant Hypertension Scientific Statement  
**APhA Reference Version:** 2024.1

---

## Blood Pressure Classification (Adults ≥18 years)

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|----------|----------------|---|-----------------|
| Normal | <120 | AND | <80 |
| Elevated | 120–129 | AND | <80 |
| Stage 1 Hypertension | 130–139 | OR | 80–89 |
| Stage 2 Hypertension | ≥140 | OR | ≥90 |
| Hypertensive Crisis | >180 | AND/OR | >120 |

*Based on average of ≥2 careful readings on ≥2 occasions*

---

## Diagnostic Considerations

### Proper Blood Pressure Measurement
- Seated, back supported, feet flat, arm at heart level
- No caffeine, exercise, or smoking 30 minutes prior
- 5 minutes rest before measurement
- Use calibrated, validated device
- Average 2+ readings per visit; confirm on separate occasions

### White Coat Hypertension
- Elevated office BP but normal out-of-office readings
- Use ABPM (24-hour) or HBPM to confirm
- Lower CV risk than sustained hypertension; may progress to true HTN

### Masked Hypertension
- Normal office BP but elevated out-of-office readings
- Higher CV risk than white coat — warrants treatment

### Home Blood Pressure Monitoring (HBPM)
- Take in morning and evening
- 2 readings per session, 1 min apart
- 7 days before appointments
- Average readings excluding first day

## Blood Pressure Treatment Targets

| Population | Target BP |
|-----------|----------|
| General adults (confirmed HTN) | <130/80 mmHg |
| Adults ≥65 years (community-dwelling) | <130/80 mmHg |
| Adults ≥65 (high fall risk, limited life expectancy) | Individualize (130–150/80–90) |
| CKD (with or without proteinuria) | <130/80 mmHg |
| Diabetes | <130/80 mmHg |
| Established CVD / High CV risk (≥10% 10-yr ASCVD) | <130/80 mmHg |
| Stroke (secondary prevention) | <130/80 mmHg |
| Pregnancy-related HTN | 130–150/80–100 mmHg (individualize) |

---

## Initial Treatment Decision Algorithm

### Stage 1 (130–139/80–89)

| 10-Year ASCVD Risk | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| <10% | Non-pharmacologic therapy alone (3–6 months trial) |
| ≥10% OR clinical CVD, CKD, diabetes, age ≥65, T10-year ASCVD risk | Lifestyle + pharmacologic therapy |

### Stage 2 (≥140/90)
- Lifestyle modifications + pharmacologic therapy regardless of ASCVD risk
- Consider 2-drug combination at initiation if BP ≥20/10 mmHg above target

---

## Non-Pharmacologic Interventions

| Intervention | Expected SBP Reduction |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Weight loss (5 kg) | -5 to -8 mmHg |
| DASH diet | -11 mmHg |
| Dietary sodium reduction (<2,300 mg/day) | -5 to -6 mmHg |
| Physical activity (aerobic) ≥90–150 min/week | -5 to -8 mmHg |
| Alcohol reduction (≤1 drink/day women; ≤2 men) | -3 to -4 mmHg |
| Smoking cessation | Overall CV benefit (not directly BP) |

## Pharmacologic Therapy

### First-Line Drug Classes (All Equally Recommended)
1. **Thiazide/thiazide-like diuretics** (chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide)
2. **ACE Inhibitors** (lisinopril, ramipril, enalapril)
3. **Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)** (losartan, valsartan, amlodipine)
4. **Calcium Channel Blockers, dihydropyridine (CCB)** (amlodipine, nifedipine ER)

*Note: ACE inhibitor + ARB combination is NOT recommended (increases adverse effects without additional BP benefit)*

### Compelling Indications — Preferred Drug Class

| Comorbidity | Preferred Agents | Avoid |
|------------|-----------------|-------|
| Post-MI | Beta-blocker, ACE inhibitor/ARB | — |
| HFrEF | ACE inhibitor/ARB (or ARNI), beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i | Non-dihydropyridine CCB |
| CKD with proteinuria | ACE inhibitor OR ARB (not both) | NSAIDs |
| Diabetes | ACE inhibitor or ARB (especially if proteinuria); any first-line for BP lowering | — |
| Stroke (secondary prevention) | ACE inhibitor + thiazide combination | — |
| Isolated systolic HTN (elderly) | Thiazide, CCB, ACE inhibitor/ARB | — |
| Pregnancy | Labetalol, nifedipine (ER), methyldopa | ACE inhibitors, ARBs, direct renin inhibitors |

### Drug Selection Considerations

**Black Patients:**
- Thiazide diuretics + CCB preferred as initial therapy (greater BP reduction as monotherapy vs. ACE inhibitors/ARBs in this population)
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs appropriate if HF, CKD, proteinuria, or diabetes present
- Higher risk of ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema

**Elderly (≥65 years):**
- Thiazides and CCBs well-tolerated; ACE inhibitors/ARBs for compelling indications
- Watch for orthostatic hypotension (measure BP supine/standing)
- Gradual titration to avoid acute hypotension/falls

### Step-Up Strategy
| Step | Therapy |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Monotherapy (ACE inhibitor or ARB OR CCB OR thiazide) |
| 2 | 2-drug combination: ACE inhibitor/ARB + CCB (preferred) OR ACE inhibitor/ARB + thiazide OR CCB + thiazide |
| 3 | 3-drug combination: ACE inhibitor/ARB + CCB + thiazide |
| 4 (Resistant HTN) | Add spironolactone or other agents |

## Resistant Hypertension

### Definition
BP above target despite 3 optimal-dose antihypertensives of different classes (including a diuretic) — after ruling out pseudo-resistance and secondary causes.

### Rule Out Pseudo-Resistance
- Poor medication adherence (most common)
- White coat effect
- Suboptimal measurement technique
- Inadequate drug doses or wrong drug class
- Drug interactions (NSAIDs, decongestants, steroids, stimulants)

### Common Secondary Causes
| Cause | Screening Test |
|-------|---------------|
| Primary aldosteronism | Plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio |
| Obstructive sleep apnea | Sleep study (polysomnography) |
| Renal artery stenosis | Duplex renal ultrasound or CTA |
| CKD | eGFR, urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio |
| Thyroid dysfunction | TSH |
| Pheochromocytoma | 24-h urine metanephrines |
| Cushing's syndrome | Overnight dexamethasone suppression test |

### Resistant HTN Treatment
- Add spironolactone 25–50 mg/day (first choice add-on)
- Alternative: eplerenone, amiloride, beta-blocker, alpha-blocker
- Refer to hypertension specialist if needed

## Hypertensive Urgency vs. Emergency

### Hypertensive Urgency
- SBP >180 or DBP >120; NO acute target organ damage
- Oral antihypertensives (captopril, labetalol, clonidine)
- Reduce BP gradually over 24–48 hours
- No evidence that immediate IV treatment improves outcomes

### Hypertensive Emergency
- SBP >180 or DBP >120 WITH acute target organ damage
- Target organs: brain (hypertensive encephalopathy, stroke), heart (ACS, acute HF), kidneys (AKI), eyes (retinal hemorrhage), aorta (dissection)
- **IV antihypertensives:** nicardipine, labetalol, clevidipine, hydralazine, esmolol, sodium nitroprusside
- Reduce MAP by ≤25% in first hour; then to 160/100–110 mmHg over next 2–6 hours
- **Exception:** Aortic dissection → lower to SBP 100–120 mmHg rapidly (IV labetalol or esmolol)

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

| Action | Timing |
|--------|--------|
| Confirm HTN diagnosis (if Stage 1, low risk) | Recheck in 1–3 months |
| After initiating new therapy | Recheck in 1 month |
| After achieving BP goal | Every 3–6 months |
| Labs (CMP, CBC, lipids, urine albumin) | Baseline; then annually |
| EKG | Baseline |
| Ophthalmology (fundoscopy) | For longstanding or poorly controlled HTN |

---

## Pharmacist's Role in Hypertension Management

- Blood pressure measurement and screening
- Medication therapy management (MTM) to optimize regimens
- Monitoring for adverse effects, drug interactions, and adherence
- Patient education on DASH diet, sodium restriction, lifestyle modification
- Collaboration with physician for dose adjustments (collaborative practice agreements in many states)
- Community pharmacy-based HTN programs (Team-Based Care model) shown to reduce BP by additional 6–8 mmHg
