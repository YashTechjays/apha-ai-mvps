# Clinical Guidelines: Pain Management

**Source:** CDC Clinical Practice Guideline for Prescribing Opioids — 2022  
**Supplemented by:** APhA Pain Management Monograph; ASPM/APS Guidelines  
**APhA Reference Version:** 2024.1

---

## Pain Classification

### By Duration
| Type | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Acute pain | <3 months; typically tissue injury-related; expected to resolve |
| Subacute pain | 3–12 weeks; transitional phase |
| Chronic pain | ≥3 months; may persist beyond healing |

### By Mechanism
| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Nociceptive (somatic) | Sharp, aching, well-localized; from tissue damage | Musculoskeletal, surgical pain |
| Nociceptive (visceral) | Diffuse, poorly localized, cramping; from organ injury | Abdominal pain, dysmenorrhea |
| Neuropathic | Burning, shooting, electric, tingling; nerve damage/dysfunction | Diabetic neuropathy, PHN, radiculopathy |
| Mixed/nociplastic | Central sensitization; no clear tissue/nerve damage | Fibromyalgia, IBS, chronic widespread pain |

## Assessment Tools

| Tool | Use |
|------|-----|
| Numeric Rating Scale (NRS 0–10) | Simple intensity rating; most widely used |
| Visual Analog Scale (VAS) | Continuous scale; research/acute settings |
| Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) | Interference with function; chronic pain |
| McGill Pain Questionnaire | Characterizes pain quality (descriptors) |
| FLACC Scale | Children <3 years or non-verbal patients |
| FACES Pain Scale (Revised) | Children ≥3 years |
| Abbey Pain Scale | Dementia patients |

**Functional assessment** is as important as pain intensity — always assess impact on sleep, activity, mood, and work/daily functioning.

## Non-Pharmacologic Therapies (First-Line for Many Conditions)

### Physical Modalities
- Heat/cold application
- Physical therapy, exercise therapy
- TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)
- Ultrasound, massage, acupuncture

### Psychological/Behavioral
- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — strong evidence for chronic pain
- Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR)
- Pain coping skills training

### Procedural
- Nerve blocks (epidural, peripheral, sympathetic)
- Trigger point injections
- Spinal cord stimulation
- Radiofrequency ablation

## Analgesic Ladder (WHO Framework)

### Step 1 — Mild Pain (NRS 1–3)
Non-opioid analgesics:
- **Acetaminophen** (APAP): 325–1,000 mg every 4–6 h; max 3,000–4,000 mg/day; reduce to ≤2,000 mg/day in liver disease/heavy alcohol use
- **NSAIDs** (see NSAID section)
- Topical analgesics (diclofenac gel, lidocaine patch, capsaicin)

### Step 2 — Moderate Pain (NRS 4–6)
- Tramadol (weak opioid + NRI): 50–100 mg every 4–6 h; max 400 mg/day; taper; abuse potential
- Low-dose opioids (codeine, hydrocodone) — use cautiously; consider jumping to Step 3 for moderate pain
- Continue non-opioid as adjunct

### Step 3 — Severe Pain (NRS 7–10)
- Strong opioids (see Opioid section)
- Continue non-opioid and adjuvant agents

### Adjuvant Analgesics (All Steps)
Used primarily for neuropathic pain:
- **Antidepressants:** Duloxetine (first-line for diabetic neuropathy, fibromyalgia), amitriptyline, nortriptyline
- **Anticonvulsants:** Gabapentin, pregabalin (neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, PHN)
- **Topical:** Lidocaine 5% patch (PHN), capsaicin (high-concentration patch 8%), diclofenac gel

## NSAIDs

### Available Agents and Dosing

| NSAID | Class | Standard Dose | OTC Availability |
|-------|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Ibuprofen | Propionic acid | 400–800 mg q6–8h (max 3,200 mg/day Rx; 1,200 mg/day OTC) | Yes |
| Naproxen | Propionic acid | 250–500 mg BID (max 1,250 mg/day; 660 mg/day OTC) | Yes |
| Naproxen sodium | — | 220–550 mg q8–12h | Yes |
| Diclofenac | Acetic acid | 50 mg TID or 75 mg BID | Topical OTC (gel) |
| Indomethacin | Acetic acid | 25–50 mg TID | No |
| Ketorolac | Acetic acid | 15–30 mg IM/IV; 10 mg oral; max 5 days | No |
| Celecoxib (COX-2) | — | 100–200 mg BID | No |
| Meloxicam (preferential COX-2) | Oxicam | 7.5–15 mg once daily | No |

### NSAID Safety Considerations

| Risk | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| GI (ulceration, bleeding) | Use lowest effective dose; shortest duration; add PPI for high-risk patients (age >65, prior GI event, anticoagulation, corticosteroids) |
| Cardiovascular (HTN, HF, MI, stroke) | Avoid in established CVD if possible; use lowest dose shortest duration; naproxen may have lower CV risk than ibuprofen among traditional NSAIDs |
| Renal (AKI, sodium retention) | Avoid in CKD (eGFR <30); use with caution in eGFR 30–60; monitor renal function |
| Platelet inhibition | Reversible (all except aspirin which is irreversible); hold before surgery |
| Pregnancy | Avoid in 3rd trimester (premature closure of ductus arteriosus); caution 2nd trimester (oligohydramnios) |

**Celecoxib vs. traditional NSAIDs:**
- Lower GI bleeding risk (comparable to naproxen + PPI)
- Similar or slightly lower CV risk at low doses
- Contains sulfonamide group (historical concern for sulfa allergy — low cross-reactivity clinically)

## Opioid Analgesics (CDC 2022 Guideline)

### Principles (CDC 2022)
1. Non-opioid therapies preferred for chronic pain
2. Opioids for acute pain: prescribe only as many tablets as needed (typically 3–7 days or less for minor injuries/procedures)
3. Establish treatment goals (functional and pain outcomes) before initiating
4. Discuss risks and benefits (addiction, overdose, driving impairment, physical dependence) — informed consent
5. Use lowest effective dose; avoid dose escalation without reassessment
6. Avoid concurrent benzodiazepines + opioids (synergistic respiratory depression)
7. Prescribe naloxone alongside opioids for higher-risk patients

### Common Opioid Agents

| Opioid | Formulations | Equianalgesic (oral) | Half-life |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Morphine (reference) | IR, ER (MS Contin), IV/SQ | 30 mg | 2–4 h (IR) |
| Oxycodone | IR (Roxicodone), ER (OxyContin) | 20 mg | 3–5 h (IR) |
| Hydrocodone | IR + APAP (Vicodin); ER (Hysingla) | 30 mg | 3–4 h |
| Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) | IR oral, IV | 7.5 mg | 2–3 h |
| Oxymorphone | IR, ER | 10 mg | 7–9 h |
| Tramadol | IR, ER | 120 mg (approximate) | 5–9 h |
| Fentanyl | Transdermal patch (Duragesic); buccal; IV | Patch mcg/h varies | Patch: 17 h (apparent) |
| Buprenorphine | SL (Belbuca), patch (Butrans); sublingual SL/SL-naloxone (Suboxone) | 0.4 mg SL ≈ 30 mg morphine | 24–72 h |
| Methadone | Oral, IV | Variable (extremely complex conversion — requires specialist) | 8–59 h |
| Codeine | IR ± APAP | 200 mg | 3 h |

### Morphine Milligram Equivalents (MME)

| Opioid | MME Conversion Factor |
|--------|----------------------|
| Codeine | × 0.15 |
| Tramadol | × 0.1 |
| Hydrocodone | × 1 |
| Morphine | × 1 |
| Oxycodone | × 1.5 |
| Hydromorphone | × 4 |
| Fentanyl patch mcg/h | × 2.4 (daily MME) |
| Buprenorphine patch mcg/h | × 12.6 (daily MME) |

**High-dose threshold (CDC 2022):** ≥50 MME/day warrants reassessment; ≥90 MME/day associated with substantially increased overdose risk.

### Opioid Risk Stratification Tools
- **ORT (Opioid Risk Tool):** 5-item questionnaire; predicts aberrant drug-related behaviors
- **PDMP (Prescription Drug Monitoring Program):** Check before prescribing; mandatory in most states; identifies overlapping prescriptions

### Naloxone Prescribing Criteria (CDC 2022)
Co-prescribe naloxone when:
- ≥50 MME/day
- Concurrent benzodiazepines or CNS depressants
- History of substance use disorder
- Mental health comorbidity
- Sleep apnea

### Opioid Use Disorder (OUD) — Medications for Addiction Treatment (MAT)

| Medication | Schedule | Dose | Setting |
|-----------|---------|------|---------|
| Methadone | Schedule II | Individualized; ≥20 mg/day typical maintenance | Opioid Treatment Program (OTP) only |
| Buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone) | Schedule III | 4/1 mg – 24/6 mg/day | Office-based (DEA waiver removed 2023) |
| Extended-release naltrexone (Vivitrol) | Non-scheduled | 380 mg IM q4 weeks | Office-based; requires full opioid-free period |

*Naloxone Hydrochloride (Narcan, Kloxxado nasal spray): 4 mg intranasal; repeat every 2–3 min as needed; onset 2–5 min; duration 30–90 min; repeat dosing may be needed for fentanyl/long-acting opioids*

## Special Populations

### Elderly (≥65 years)
- Start with non-opioid and non-pharmacologic approaches
- Avoid: Ketorolac, indomethacin (high GI/renal risk), meperidine (Demerol — toxic metabolite normeperidine, risk of seizures), propoxyphene (withdrawn)
- If opioids needed: "start low, go slow"; monitor for falls, constipation, cognitive impairment
- **Beers Criteria 2023:** Caution with NSAIDs (GI bleeding, renal), opioids (fall risk, cognitive), muscle relaxants (anticholinergic, sedation)

### Renal Impairment
| Drug | eGFR threshold | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| NSAIDs (all) | <30 mL/min | Avoid |
| Morphine | Severe impairment | Avoid (active metabolite M6G accumulates) |
| Codeine | Severe impairment | Avoid (codeine-6-glucuronide accumulates) |
| Hydromorphone | Severe impairment | Use with caution |
| Oxycodone | Severe impairment | Reduce dose; monitor |
| Fentanyl/buprenorphine | Any | Safest options in CKD |

### Cancer Pain
- WHO analgesic ladder applies
- Combine scheduled long-acting opioid + PRN short-acting for breakthrough
- Address anxiety, depression, spiritual distress (total pain model)
- Palliative care consultation for complex cases
