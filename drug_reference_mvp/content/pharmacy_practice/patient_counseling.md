# Patient Counseling: Best Practices

**Source:** APhA Patient Counseling Resource Guide; OBRA-90 Mandate  
**APhA Reference Version:** 2024.1

---

## Legal Framework

### OBRA-90 (Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1990)
Federal law requiring pharmacists to offer counseling for Medicaid patients on new prescriptions. Most states have extended this to ALL patients via state pharmacy practice acts.

### Required Counseling Elements (OBRA-90)
1. Name and description of the medication
2. Dosage, dosage form, route, and duration
3. Special directions and precautions
4. Common adverse effects and how to manage them
5. Techniques for self-monitoring
6. Proper storage
7. Refill information
8. Action to take in case of missed dose

## The Indian Health Service 3-Prime Questions Method

A patient-centered counseling approach used in pharmacy:

### For NEW Prescriptions
1. **"What did your doctor tell you the medication is for?"**
   - Confirms understanding of purpose
2. **"How did your doctor tell you to take the medication?"**
   - Verifies understanding of directions
3. **"What did your doctor tell you to expect?"**
   - Assesses awareness of side effects and outcomes

### For REFILLS
1. **"What do you take the medication for?"**
2. **"How do you take it?"**
3. **"What kind of problems are you having?"**

### Counseling Closure: The Final Verification
Ask the patient to explain back the key points in their own words ("teach-back method"):
- "Just to make sure I explained everything clearly, can you tell me how you'll take this medication?"
- Identifies misunderstandings before patient leaves

## Counseling Communication Principles

### The "5 Rs" of Pharmacy Counseling
1. **Relationship** — Establish rapport; introduce yourself; private setting
2. **Reason** — Explain why the medication is being prescribed
3. **Regimen** — Explain how, when, and how much to take
4. **Results** — Set expectations for therapeutic outcome and timeline
5. **Risks** — Discuss adverse effects and warning signs

### Health Literacy Considerations
- Use plain language; avoid medical jargon ("water pill" instead of "diuretic")
- 6th-grade reading level for written materials
- Visual aids (pill cards, calendars, demonstration devices)
- Confirm understanding with teach-back, not "Do you understand?"
- Address cultural/language barriers (offer interpreter if available)
- Sensitivity to limited literacy without making patient feel embarrassed

### SOLER Body Language (Egan)
- **S**quare body posture facing patient
- **O**pen body posture (no crossed arms)
- **L**ean slightly forward
- **E**ye contact (culturally appropriate)
- **R**elaxed posture

### Active Listening
- Reflect back what patient says
- Validate concerns ("I can see why that worries you")
- Avoid interrupting
- Use silence appropriately to allow patient to process

## Adherence: Drivers and Interventions

### Common Causes of Non-Adherence (WHO 5 Dimensions)
1. **Patient factors:** Forgetfulness, motivation, beliefs about illness/medication, health literacy
2. **Social/economic factors:** Cost, transportation, family/social support, employment
3. **Healthcare team/system:** Communication, continuity of care, access
4. **Condition-related:** Asymptomatic disease (HTN, dyslipidemia), comorbidities, mental health
5. **Therapy-related:** Complex regimens, polypharmacy, side effects, duration of therapy

### Evidence-Based Adherence Interventions

| Intervention | Target Issue |
|--------------|-------------|
| Pill organizers, blister packs | Forgetfulness; complex regimen |
| Once-daily formulations | Regimen simplification |
| Combination products | Pill burden reduction |
| Auto-refill, mail-order, 90-day supplies | Convenience; cost |
| Medication Therapy Management (MTM) | Polypharmacy; chronic disease |
| Text/phone reminders | Forgetfulness |
| Generic substitution | Cost |
| Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs) | Cost |
| Mobile apps (Medisafe, MyTherapy) | Tracking, reminders |
| Motivational interviewing | Ambivalence about therapy |

### Measuring Adherence
- **Proportion of Days Covered (PDC)** — Quality measure used by CMS Star Ratings; ≥80% = adherent
- **Medication Possession Ratio (MPR)** — Older measure; similar concept
- **Pill counts** — Direct but cumbersome
- **Self-report (MMAS-8 Morisky Scale)** — Subjective; over-estimates adherence
- **Refill history** — Easy access; can mask non-use after fill

## Counseling for Specific Drug Classes

### Inhalers (MDI, DPI, Soft Mist)

#### MDI (Metered Dose Inhaler — e.g., albuterol HFA, fluticasone HFA)
1. Remove cap; shake well
2. Prime if first use or unused for ≥2 weeks
3. Exhale fully (away from mouthpiece)
4. Place mouthpiece between teeth; close lips around it
5. Begin slow, deep inhalation; press canister at start of inhalation
6. Continue slow inhalation (3–5 seconds total)
7. Hold breath 5–10 seconds
8. Exhale slowly
9. Wait 30–60 seconds between puffs
10. For corticosteroids: rinse mouth and spit to prevent thrush

**Spacer use:** Recommended for all patients but especially children, elderly, those with poor coordination — improves drug delivery and reduces oropharyngeal deposition

#### DPI (Dry Powder Inhaler — e.g., Advair Diskus, Symbicort, Spiriva HandiHaler)
1. Load dose per device-specific instructions
2. Exhale fully (away from mouthpiece — moisture clumps powder)
3. Place mouthpiece in mouth; seal with lips
4. Inhale forcefully and deeply (rapid inhalation needed to disperse powder)
5. Hold breath 5–10 seconds
6. Rinse mouth if corticosteroid

**Common DPI errors:**
- Exhaling into mouthpiece (humidity ruins next dose)
- Inhaling too slowly
- Not fully loading the dose

#### Respimat (Soft Mist Inhaler)
- Slower mist allows easier coordination than MDI
- Inhale slowly and deeply (different from DPI)

### Insulin Injection Counseling

#### Pen Devices (Lantus, NovoLog FlexPen, etc.)
1. Wash hands; gather supplies
2. Attach new needle; remove outer/inner needle caps
3. Prime with 2 units, expel into air — visualize drop (do every time)
4. Dial dose
5. Inject sites: abdomen (avoid 2-inch perimeter around navel), thighs, upper arms, buttocks — ROTATE sites
6. Pinch skin; insert needle 90° (or 45° for thin individuals)
7. Press plunger; count to 10 with needle in (full dose delivery)
8. Withdraw needle; do not rub site
9. Dispose of needle in sharps container

#### Insulin Storage
- Unopened: refrigerator (do not freeze)
- Opened pen/vial: room temperature (28–30 days, product-dependent); some refrigerated
- Avoid extreme heat (in car, direct sunlight)
- Discard if cloudy when should be clear, or vice versa

### Eye Drop Administration
1. Wash hands
2. Tilt head back; pull lower eyelid down to form a pocket
3. Hold dropper above eye; do not touch dropper to eye/lashes
4. Squeeze 1 drop into pocket
5. Close eye gently 1–2 minutes; do not squeeze
6. Apply pressure to inner corner (punctal occlusion) to reduce systemic absorption
7. Wait 5 minutes between different eye drops
8. Suspensions: shake first
9. Replace cap immediately

### Topical Patches (Fentanyl, Nicotine, Estradiol, Scopolamine, Clonidine)
- Apply to clean, dry, hairless skin
- Rotate sites with each application
- Press firmly for 30 seconds
- Wash hands after handling (especially fentanyl — risk to handlers)
- Do not cut patches (extended-release matrix disrupted)
- Used patches still contain drug — fold sticky sides together and dispose properly
- Fentanyl patches: flush down toilet if no take-back program (FDA exception due to fatality risk)

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Counseling
- Counsel BOTH parent and age-appropriate child
- Use child's name; explain at developmental level
- Liquid medications: provide and demonstrate dosing syringe
- Discuss palatability; offer mixing tips (avoid grapefruit juice, dairy with antibiotics)
- Storage: out of reach; childproof caps

### Geriatric Counseling
- Slow pace; speak clearly (not necessarily loud)
- Verify hearing/vision aids in use
- Provide large-print materials
- Simplify regimen when possible
- Involve caregivers when appropriate (with patient permission)
- Address polypharmacy
- Review high-risk medications (Beers Criteria)

### Pregnancy and Lactation
- Use LactMed database (NIH) for breastfeeding compatibility
- Discuss benefit/risk; encourage shared decision-making
- Avoid blanket "do not take in pregnancy" — many medications are safer than untreated condition
- Folic acid for women of childbearing potential
- Refer to maternal-fetal medicine specialist for complex cases

### Limited English Proficiency (LEP)
- Use professional interpreters (in-person, telephone, video)
- Avoid using family members for sensitive medical information
- Provide written materials in patient's language when available
- Verify understanding through teach-back

## Difficult Counseling Situations

### Patient Refuses Counseling
- Document refusal
- Provide written information
- Offer to counsel by phone later

### Suspected Diversion or Drug-Seeking Behavior
- Verify PDMP
- Communicate with prescriber
- Document objective observations only
- Do not accuse patient

### Adverse Drug Reaction Disclosure
- Acknowledge concern
- Verify reaction (true allergy vs. side effect vs. intolerance)
- Update allergy profile
- Communicate with prescriber for therapy change
- Report to MedWatch if appropriate

### Cost Barriers
- Generic substitution
- Therapeutic alternatives (with prescriber approval)
- Manufacturer coupons; patient assistance programs (PAPs)
- 340B pricing (eligible institutions)
- GoodRx, Cost Plus Drugs, NeedyMeds resources

## Documentation of Counseling

Key elements:
- Date and time
- Pharmacist signature
- Medications counseled
- Topics covered
- Patient understanding (assessment outcomes)
- Follow-up needed
- Any referrals made

State board rules vary — verify local requirements.
