# Lisinopril

**Drug Class:** Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitor  
**Brand Names:** Prinivil, Zestril, Qbrelis (oral solution)  
**APhA Reference Version:** 2024.1

---

## Mechanism of Action

Lisinopril competitively inhibits angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), which converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II. Reduced angiotensin II leads to vasodilation (reduced SVR), decreased aldosterone secretion (natriuresis, reduced preload), and decreased vasopressin release. Bradykinin accumulation (ACE also degrades bradykinin) contributes to both therapeutic effects and the characteristic cough.

---

## Indications

- **FDA-Approved:**
  - Hypertension (adults and pediatric ≥6 years)
  - Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF)
  - Acute myocardial infarction (within 24 hours of symptom onset, hemodynamically stable)
  - Diabetic nephropathy (off-label but guideline-supported)

---

## Dosing

### Hypertension — Adults
- Start: 10 mg once daily (5 mg if on diuretic, renal impairment, or hyponatremia)
- Usual maintenance: 20–40 mg/day
- Maximum: 80 mg/day

### Hypertension — Pediatric (≥6 years)
- Start: 0.07 mg/kg/day (max 5 mg initial dose)
- Titrate based on response; max 0.61 mg/kg/day or 40 mg/day

### Heart Failure
- Start: 2.5–5 mg once daily
- Target: 20–40 mg/day
- Titrate every 2 weeks as tolerated

### Acute MI
- 5 mg within 24 hours of MI, then 5 mg at 24 h, 10 mg at 48 h, then 10 mg daily for 6 weeks (or long-term if indicated)
- Use 2.5 mg initial dose if systolic BP 100–120 mmHg

### Renal Dose Adjustment
| CrCl (mL/min) | Starting Dose |
|----------------|---------------|
| >30 | 10 mg/day |
| 10–30 | 5 mg/day |
| <10 (including dialysis) | 2.5 mg/day; supplement on dialysis days |

---

## Contraindications

- History of ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema
- Hereditary or idiopathic angioedema
- Concurrent use with aliskiren in patients with diabetes
- Concurrent use with sacubitril/valsartan (Entresto) — do not initiate within 36 hours of switching

**Black Box Warning — Pregnancy:** Can cause fetal injury and death. Discontinue immediately when pregnancy is detected.

---

## Warnings and Precautions

### Angioedema
Can involve face, lips, tongue, glottis, larynx (potentially fatal). Occurs more frequently in Black patients. Discontinue immediately; do NOT re-challenge.

### Hypotension
Most common at initiation, especially in volume-depleted patients or those on high-dose diuretics. Start low; monitor BP after first dose.

### Hyperkalemia
Risk increased with renal impairment, diabetes, K+ supplements, K+-sparing diuretics, or NSAIDs. Monitor potassium.

### Renal Impairment
Can cause acute kidney injury, particularly in renal artery stenosis (bilateral or solitary kidney). Monitor SCr and electrolytes.

### Cough
Dry, persistent cough in 5–15% of patients (higher incidence in women and Asian patients). Caused by bradykinin accumulation. If intolerable, switch to an ARB.

---

## Drug Interactions

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Management |
|-----------------|--------|------------|
| NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors | Reduced antihypertensive effect; increased renal impairment risk | Avoid; use acetaminophen if needed |
| K+-sparing diuretics, K+ supplements, trimethoprim | Hyperkalemia | Monitor potassium closely |
| Lithium | Increased lithium levels | Monitor lithium levels |
| Aliskiren | Increased risk of hypotension, hyperkalemia, renal impairment | Contraindicated in diabetes; avoid in renal impairment |
| Sacubitril/valsartan | Angioedema risk | Allow 36-hour washout |
| Gold salts | Nitritoid reactions (flushing, nausea, hypotension) | Use caution |
| Diuretics | Additive hypotension | Reduce diuretic or start ACE at low dose |

---

## Adverse Effects

| Effect | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Dry cough | 5–15% |
| Dizziness/hypotension | Common at initiation |
| Hyperkalemia | Common in at-risk patients |
| Angioedema | 0.1–0.7% |
| Acute kidney injury | Rare; risk in renal artery stenosis |
| Rash/taste disturbance | Uncommon |

---

## Pharmacokinetics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Bioavailability | ~25% (oral) |
| Tmax | 7 h |
| Protein binding | <10% |
| Metabolism | Not metabolized; excreted unchanged |
| Half-life | ~12 h (prolonged in renal impairment) |
| Elimination | Renal (exclusively) |

---

## Monitoring Parameters

- Blood pressure (response and tolerability)
- Renal function (SCr, BUN) — at baseline, 1–2 weeks after initiation or dose change
- Serum potassium — same schedule as renal function
- Signs of angioedema
- Symptoms of hypotension (dizziness, lightheadedness)

---

## Patient Counseling Points

1. Take at the same time each day; may be taken with or without food.
2. A dry cough is a common side effect — contact your pharmacist/physician if bothersome; do not stop on your own.
3. Stop immediately and seek emergency care for any swelling of face, lips, tongue, or throat.
4. Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) without consulting your provider.
5. Do not take potassium supplements or salt substitutes (containing K+) without medical guidance.
6. Women of childbearing age: notify your provider immediately if you become pregnant.
7. Rise slowly from sitting or lying position to prevent dizziness (orthostatic hypotension).

---

## Special Populations

| Population | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| Pregnancy | **CONTRAINDICATED** (Category D/X in 2nd and 3rd trimesters) — causes fetal renal dysplasia |
| Lactation | Small amounts in breast milk; generally avoid in newborns/premature infants |
| Elderly | Higher risk of hypotension and hyperkalemia; start low |
| Black patients | Higher angioedema risk; less BP reduction as monotherapy vs. other races — consider adding thiazide |
| Renal artery stenosis | Risk of acute renal failure — use with extreme caution if bilateral |
