## High-Alert Medications (ISMP)

These medications carry heightened risk of causing significant patient harm when used in error. Extra safeguards required.

### Acute Care High-Alert List
| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Anticoagulants | Warfarin, heparin, LMWH, DOACs, argatroban |
| Insulin (all formulations) | Subcutaneous and IV |
| Opioids (IV, transdermal, oral concentrate) | Morphine, hydromorphone, fentanyl |
| Chemotherapeutic agents | Methotrexate, vincristine, cyclophosphamide |
| Concentrated electrolytes | KCl, NaCl >0.9%, magnesium sulfate >40 mg/mL, phosphates |
| Neuromuscular blocking agents | Vecuronium, rocuronium, succinylcholine |
| Sedation agents (moderate/deep) | Propofol, midazolam |
| Adrenergic agents | Epinephrine, norepinephrine, phenylephrine IV |
| Sterile water injection | ≥100 mL containers |
| Hypoglycemics (oral) | Sulfonylureas |

### Community/Ambulatory High-Alert List
- Anticoagulants (oral and parenteral)
- Insulins (all)
- Opioids (oral and transdermal)
- Methotrexate (oral) — especially confusion with daily vs. weekly dosing
- Sulfonylureas
- Pediatric liquid medications

### Safety Strategies for High-Alert Medications
1. Limit access to concentrated electrolytes
2. Standardize order sets, concentrations, dosing
3. Use independent double-checks
4. Bar-code medication administration (BCMA)
5. Use smart pumps with dose error reduction systems (DERS)
6. Removable warning labels (e.g., "HIGH ALERT")

## Medication Reconciliation

### Definition (Joint Commission NPSG)
The process of comparing a patient's medication orders to all medications the patient has been taking. Reduces errors at transitions of care: admission, transfer, discharge.

### 5-Step Process
1. **Develop list of current medications** (BPMH — Best Possible Medication History)
2. **Develop list of medications to be prescribed** (new orders)
3. **Compare the two lists** — identify discrepancies (intentional vs. unintentional)
4. **Make clinical decisions** based on comparison
5. **Communicate** the new list to providers, patient, next setting

### BPMH Sources
- Patient/family interview (always primary if possible)
- Pill bottles, medication lists from patient
- Pharmacy refill records (PMP, community pharmacy)
- Previous discharge summaries
- Referring provider/specialist
- Electronic Health Record (EHR) review
- Long-term care facility records

### Pharmacist's Role
- Most effective conductor of medication reconciliation (Studies show pharmacist-led med rec reduces errors by 50–80% vs. nurse or physician-led)
- Reduce 30-day hospital readmissions
- Identify adherence barriers, simplify regimens
- Discharge counseling

## Pharmacovigilance and Adverse Drug Event Reporting

### MedWatch (FDA)
Voluntary reporting system for adverse drug events:
- Submit at www.fda.gov/medwatch
- Form FDA 3500 (voluntary; for healthcare professionals)
- Form FDA 3500B (consumer/patient)

### When to Report
- **Serious outcomes:** Death, life-threatening event, hospitalization, persistent disability, congenital anomaly, requires intervention to prevent permanent damage
- Unexpected adverse events (not in labeling)
- Therapeutic failures
- Quality problems (product defects)

### Common Pitfalls Pharmacists Should Watch For
1. **Acetaminophen toxicity:** Combination products (Vicodin, Percocet, Norco) contain APAP — exceed 4 g/day risk
2. **Anticoagulant errors:** Failure to bridge or hold appropriately; conversion errors between agents
3. **Insulin errors:** U-100 vs. U-500; basal vs. mealtime mix-ups; sliding scale calculation errors
4. **Pediatric weight-based dosing:** Verify weight; double-check calculations
5. **Renal dosing missed:** Check eGFR/CrCl before dispensing renally-cleared meds
6. **Opioid prescribing:** Total MME calculations; concurrent benzodiazepines; PDMP check
7. **Methotrexate weekly dosing errors:** Confused with daily dosing → fatal toxicity

## Beers Criteria — Potentially Inappropriate Medications in Older Adults (2023 Update)

### Categories of Concern
1. **Medications to avoid in older adults regardless of condition**
2. **Medications to avoid in older adults with specific conditions**
3. **Medications to use with caution**
4. **Drug-drug interactions to avoid**
5. **Renal dose adjustments needed**

### High-Yield Avoid List

| Drug/Class | Concern | Alternative |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine, chlorpheniramine) | Anticholinergic, sedation, cognitive impairment | Second-gen (loratadine, cetirizine, fexofenadine) |
| Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, doxepin >6 mg/day) | Anticholinergic, sedation, orthostatic hypotension | SSRIs (sertraline preferred), nortriptyline if TCA needed |
| Benzodiazepines (all) | Falls, cognitive impairment, dependence | Non-benzodiazepine sleep aids; CBT-I |
| Non-benzodiazepine "Z-drugs" (zolpidem, eszopiclone, zaleplon) | Falls; cognitive impairment | Sleep hygiene; CBT-I |
| NSAIDs (chronic use) | GI bleeding, AKI, CV events | Acetaminophen, topical NSAIDs, non-pharm |
| Skeletal muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol) | Anticholinergic, sedation | Physical therapy; topical therapies |
| Glyburide | Prolonged hypoglycemia | Glipizide or DPP-4i, GLP-1 RA, SGLT2i |
| Megestrol | Thrombosis | Nutrition support |
| Estrogens (oral/transdermal) | CV/cancer risk in elderly | Topical vaginal estrogen acceptable |
| Digoxin >0.125 mg/day | Toxicity risk | Lower doses; monitor levels |
| Sliding scale insulin (alone, without basal) | Higher hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia variability | Basal/bolus regimen |
| Proton pump inhibitors (>8 weeks) | C. diff, fractures, B12, magnesium deficiency | Time-limited use; H2RA alternative |

## Black Box Warnings: Pharmacist Vigilance

Selected high-impact black box warnings to recognize:

| Drug/Class | Warning |
|-----------|---------|
| Fluoroquinolones | Tendon rupture, peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects, aortic aneurysm |
| All SSRIs/SNRIs/atypical antidepressants | Suicidality in children/young adults |
| Metformin | Lactic acidosis (renal impairment, alcohol, hypoxic states) |
| All antipsychotics in dementia | Increased mortality in elderly with dementia-related psychosis |
| Bupropion, varenicline | Neuropsychiatric symptoms (warning removed 2016; product changes ongoing) |
| Long-acting beta agonists (LABA) — alone in asthma | Asthma-related death |
| Pioglitazone | Bladder cancer (with long-term use); congestive heart failure |
| Rosiglitazone | CV ischemic events |
| TNF-alpha inhibitors | Serious infections, malignancy |
| Methotrexate | Multiple severe toxicities; **weekly dosing only** for rheumatologic conditions |
| Warfarin | Bleeding |
| Codeine in pediatrics | Death in CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizers post-tonsillectomy |
| Tramadol in pediatrics | Same concern as codeine |

## Pharmacy-Specific Safety Practices

### Verification Steps for Dispensing
1. Verify prescription is complete (drug, dose, route, frequency, duration, indication, prescriber)
2. Patient identification (2 identifiers: name + DOB or name + address)
3. Check allergies and previous adverse reactions
4. Drug interaction screening
5. Therapeutic appropriateness for indication, age, weight, renal/hepatic function
6. NDC verification at multiple points (RX entry, fill, verification)
7. Final verification by pharmacist
8. Patient counseling at dispensing

### Pediatric Dosing Safety
- Weight in kilograms — verify recent weight; reject if missing
- Use mg/kg dosing; verify calculations
- Do not exceed adult maximum doses
- Dosing syringes (not household teaspoons) for liquid medications
- Concentration clarity (e.g., "amoxicillin 250 mg/5 mL" vs. "amoxicillin 400 mg/5 mL")

### Verbal/Telephone Order Safety
- Read back and confirm (closed-loop communication)
- Spell out drug names if unclear (especially LASA pairs)
- Avoid in non-urgent situations; require written/electronic order
- Document name of prescriber and authentication later

## Counseling: USP <795> and <797> Compendia (Compounding)

### USP <795> — Non-Sterile Compounding
- Ingredient identity and quality
- Beyond-use dating (BUD) standards
- Documentation requirements
- Personnel training and competency

### USP <797> — Sterile Compounding
- ISO classification of cleanrooms
- Risk levels (Low, Medium, High)
- Garbing, hand hygiene, aseptic technique
- BUD by category and storage conditions
- Quality assurance and air sampling

### USP <800> — Hazardous Drug Handling
- Hazardous Drug List (NIOSH)
- Containment Primary Engineering Controls (CPEC)
- PPE requirements
- Spill management
