# Drug Interactions: Clinical Reference Guide

**Source:** APhA Drug Interactions Handbook; Lexicomp; Micromedex  
**APhA Reference Version:** 2024.1

## Understanding Drug Interaction Classifications

### Severity Categories
| Level | Definition | Action |
|-------|------------|--------|
| **Contraindicated** | Concurrent use should not occur under any circumstances | Do not use together |
| **Major** | Life-threatening or permanent damage possible; benefit must clearly outweigh risk | Avoid combination; use only if benefits outweigh serious risks |
| **Moderate** | Exacerbation of patient condition; additional monitoring required | Use with caution; monitor |
| **Minor** | Minimally affected; limited clinical effect | Be aware; rarely needs intervention |

### Types of Drug Interactions
1. **Pharmacokinetic (PK):** Absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion altered
2. **Pharmacodynamic (PD):** Additive, synergistic, or antagonistic pharmacological effects
3. **Pharmaceutical (compatibility):** Physical/chemical incompatibility (IV admixtures)

## CYP450 Enzyme System: Key Interactions

### CYP3A4 (Most Common — ~50% of drugs metabolized)

#### Strong CYP3A4 Inhibitors (↑ substrate levels)
| Drug | Category | Clinical Significance |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| Clarithromycin | Antibiotic (macrolide) | Significant; many serious interactions |
| Itraconazole | Antifungal (azole) | Significant |
| Ketoconazole | Antifungal (azole) | Strong; avoid broad substrate use |
| Ritonavir | HIV protease inhibitor | Extremely strong; used therapeutically to "boost" other PIs |
| Cobicistat | Pharmacokinetic enhancer | Similar to ritonavir |
| Nefazodone | Antidepressant | Withdrawn in US due to hepatotoxicity |
| Grapefruit juice | Food | Moderate strength; intestinal CYP3A4 only |

#### Strong CYP3A4 Inducers (↓ substrate levels)
| Drug | Category |
|------|----------|
| Rifampin | Antibiotic |
| Phenytoin | Anticonvulsant |
| Carbamazepine | Anticonvulsant |
| Phenobarbital | Barbiturate |
| St. John's Wort | Herbal supplement |
| Efavirenz | HIV NNRTI |

#### Key CYP3A4 Substrates
Statins (lovastatin, simvastatin >> atorvastatin, rosuvastatin), cyclosporine, tacrolimus, midazolam, alprazolam, fentanyl, methadone, amiodarone, many HIV antiretrovirals, calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, diltiazem), colchicine, sildenafil

### CYP2D6

#### Inhibitors
| Drug | Strength |
|------|---------|
| Fluoxetine | Strong |
| Paroxetine | Strong |
| Bupropion | Moderate |
| Duloxetine | Moderate |
| Sertraline | Moderate (at higher doses) |
| Terbinafine | Strong |
| Quinidine | Strong |
| Amiodarone | Moderate |

#### Key CYP2D6 Substrates
Codeine → morphine (CYP2D6 converts to active form; poor metabolizers get no analgesia; ultra-rapid metabolizers at toxicity risk), tramadol, metoprolol, carvedilol, propafenone, TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine), haloperidol, risperidone, aripiprazole, tamoxifen → active endoxifen

#### Clinical Pearl: Codeine + SSRI Interaction
- **Fluoxetine/paroxetine** + codeine → inhibit CYP2D6 → reduced conversion of codeine to morphine → loss of analgesic efficacy
- **Ultra-rapid metabolizers** of CYP2D6 → excessive conversion of codeine → morphine toxicity (fatal cases in pediatrics)
- Codeine is generally avoided in children <12 years and post-tonsillectomy/adenoidectomy due to variable CYP2D6 activity

### CYP2C9

#### Inhibitors
| Drug | Strength |
|------|---------|
| Fluconazole | Strong |
| Amiodarone | Moderate |
| Miconazole (topical/vaginal) | Significant for warfarin |
| Metronidazole | Moderate |

#### Key Substrates
Warfarin (S-enantiomer), NSAIDs (celecoxib, ibuprofen, meloxicam), phenytoin, glipizide, glimepiride, losartan

#### CYP2C19

**Inhibitors:** Omeprazole, esomeprazole (strong), fluoxetine, fluvoxamine  
**Inducers:** Rifampin  
**Substrates:** Clopidogrel (prodrug to active thiol → impaired by PPI use), PPIs themselves, voriconazole, diazepam, amitriptyline, escitalopram

**Clinical Pearl: Clopidogrel + PPI**
- CYP2C19 inhibitors (omeprazole, esomeprazole) reduce conversion of clopidogrel to active metabolite
- Pantoprazole is the preferred PPI with clopidogrel (least CYP2C19 inhibition)
- FDA advisory to avoid omeprazole; however, clinical significance continues to be debated
- Benefit of GI protection may outweigh antiplatelet interaction risk in high GI bleed risk patients

## Serotonin Syndrome: Key Drug Combinations

### Serotonergic Agents (Risk When Combined)
| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| SSRIs | Fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine, citalopram, escitalopram |
| SNRIs | Venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine |
| TCAs | Clomipramine, imipramine |
| MAOIs | Phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline, linezolid, methylene blue |
| Opioids | Tramadol, meperidine, fentanyl, tapentadol |
| Triptans | Sumatriptan, rizatriptan |
| Antiemetics | Ondansetron, metoclopramide (weak) |
| Dextromethorphan (DXM) | OTC cough suppressants |
| Lithium | — |
| St. John's Wort | — |
| Linezolid | Antibiotic (MAO inhibition) |

### Clinical Presentation (Hunter Criteria)
One or more of:
- **Clonus** (spontaneous, inducible, ocular) — most specific
- Agitation
- Diaphoresis
- Tremor
- Hyperreflexia
- Hyperthermia (severe cases)

### Management
- Discontinue offending agents
- Supportive care (IV fluids, cooling)
- Benzodiazepines for agitation/seizures
- Cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist): 12 mg initial, then 2 mg q2h if needed
- ICU admission for severe cases

## QT Prolongation Risk Combinations

### High-Risk QT-Prolonging Drugs

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Antiarrhythmics | Amiodarone, sotalol, quinidine, procainamide, dofetilide |
| Antipsychotics | Haloperidol, ziprasidone, quetiapine, chlorpromazine |
| Antidepressants | Citalopram (dose-dependent), escitalopram, tricyclics |
| Antibiotics | Fluoroquinolones (levofloxacin > ciprofloxacin), macrolides (azithromycin, clarithromycin) |
| Antihistamines | Terfenadine, astemizole (withdrawn from US market for this reason) |
| Antimalarials | Chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine |
| Antifungals | Fluconazole, voriconazole |
| Others | Methadone (very high risk), ondansetron (>32 mg IV), domperidone |

### Risk Factors for Drug-Induced QT Prolongation
- Female sex (longer baseline QTc)
- Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia
- Bradycardia
- Structural heart disease
- Congenital long QT syndrome
- Drug combinations (additive QT prolongation)
- High drug concentrations (CYP inhibitor combinations)

### Prevention
- Obtain baseline ECG when initiating high-risk agents
- Correct electrolytes before and during treatment
- Avoid combinations of QT-prolonging drugs when possible
- Monitor QTc during therapy (if >500 ms, consider discontinuation; if increase >60 ms, reassess)

## Bleeding Risk Combinations

### High Bleeding Risk Drug Combinations

| Combination | Risk | Management |
|------------|------|-----------|
| Warfarin + aspirin | GI and intracranial bleeding ↑ | Avoid if possible; use if necessary (mechanical valve, post-ACS) + PPI |
| DOAC + aspirin | Major bleeding ↑ ~50% | Limit aspirin use; consider dual therapy carefully post-ACS/PCI |
| DOAC + NSAID | GI and major bleeding | Avoid; use acetaminophen for pain |
| Warfarin + NSAID | GI bleeding, altered INR | Avoid; use COX-2 or acetaminophen |
| SSRI + NSAID | GI bleeding ↑ ~3–15× | Add PPI if combination necessary |
| Antiplatelet + antiplatelet (aspirin + clopidogrel) | GI and bleeding ↑ | Limited to specific indications (ACS, post-PCI); add PPI |
| Triple therapy (anticoagulant + 2 antiplatelets) | Substantial bleeding risk | Reserve for high-stent thrombosis risk; use limited duration (1–4 weeks) |

## Hypoglycemia Risk Combinations

| Combination | Mechanism | Management |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| Sulfonylurea + fluoroquinolones (gatifloxacin > ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) | Stimulates insulin secretion | Prefer beta-lactam antibiotics; monitor glucose |
| Insulin + beta-blockers | Masks tachycardia (hypoglycemia symptom); prolongs hypoglycemia | Use cardioselective beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol) with caution; counsel patient |
| Insulin/SU + alcohol | Impairs gluconeogenesis; masks symptoms | Counsel: eat when drinking; monitor glucose |
| MAOIs + insulin/SU | Enhance hypoglycemic effect | Avoid combination or monitor very closely |

## Drug-Food Interactions

| Drug | Food/Substance | Effect | Management |
|------|---------------|--------|-----------|
| Warfarin | Vitamin K (leafy greens, green tea) | ↓ INR | Consistent intake; avoid sudden changes |
| Warfarin | Grapefruit (minor) | Slight ↑ warfarin | Minimal clinical significance |
| Statins (lovastatin, simvastatin, atorvastatin) | Grapefruit juice (>250 mL/day) | ↑ statin levels (CYP3A4 inhibition in gut) | Avoid large quantities |
| Sildenafil/PDE5 inhibitors | Grapefruit | ↑ drug levels | Avoid |
| MAOIs | Tyramine-rich foods (aged cheese, cured meats, wine, beer, overripe fruits) | Hypertensive crisis | Strict dietary restriction; avoid foods listed |
| Calcium channel blockers | Grapefruit | ↑ levels | Avoid or take at different times |
| Fluoroquinolones | Dairy, calcium-fortified foods, antacids (within 2 h) | ↓ absorption (chelation) | Separate by ≥2 h (quinolone before dairy/antacid) |
| Tetracyclines/doxycycline | Dairy, calcium, antacids, iron | ↓ absorption | Take 1–2 h before or 2–3 h after |
| Bisphosphonates (alendronate) | Food, beverages (other than water), minerals | ↓ absorption | Take on empty stomach with full glass of water; wait 30 min before eating |
| Metformin | Alcohol | ↑ lactic acidosis risk | Minimize alcohol |
| Levodopa/carbidopa | High-protein meal | Competes with amino acid transport | Distribute protein evenly throughout day |
| Thyroid hormone (levothyroxine) | Calcium, iron, soy, high-fiber meals | ↓ absorption | Take 30–60 min before breakfast; separate from supplements by 4 h |

## Drug-Herbal/Supplement Interactions

| Supplement | Interacting Drug | Effect | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|-----------|
| St. John's Wort | Warfarin | ↓ INR (bleeding risk) | CYP3A4/PgP induction |
| St. John's Wort | OCP (oral contraceptives) | Contraceptive failure | CYP3A4 induction |
| St. John's Wort | SSRIs, SNRIs, triptans | Serotonin syndrome | Serotonergic activity |
| St. John's Wort | Cyclosporine | Transplant rejection | CYP3A4 induction |
| Fish oil (high dose, >3 g/day) | Warfarin, antiplatelet agents | ↑ bleeding | Antiplatelet effect |
| Garlic (high dose) | Anticoagulants | ↑ bleeding | Antiplatelet + anticoagulant activity |
| Ginkgo biloba | Anticoagulants, antiplatelets | ↑ bleeding | Antiplatelet |
| Ginseng | Warfarin | ↓ INR | Unknown mechanism |
| Kava | Benzodiazepines, alcohol, CNS depressants | ↑ CNS depression | Additive |
| Valerian | Benzodiazepines | ↑ sedation | GABA-A receptor activity |
| Echinacea | Hepatotoxic drugs | ↑ hepatotoxicity | Hepatic concerns |
| Melatonin | Anticoagulants | Possible ↑ bleeding | Antiplatelet activity |
| Black cohosh | Estrogen, tamoxifen | Potential estrogenic effects | Phytoestrogenic activity |
